Why Composable Architecture Matters

Updated: May 7, 2026
Author
Martijn van Deel

Composable architecture matters because digital platforms need to change faster than traditional platform landscapes allow. It reduces friction by breaking customer-facing platforms into modular capabilities that can be selected, connected, replaced, and evolved more independently.

Customer expectations keep moving. New channels appear. Ecommerce journeys become more personalised. AI and automation create new service opportunities. Marketplaces, partner integrations, content platforms, customer data, payment services, search, and fulfilment capabilities all need to evolve at different speeds.

When those capabilities are locked inside one large monolith, every change becomes heavier than it should be. A small customer-facing improvement can become a cross-platform release. A new service can require workarounds in the core. Replacing one weak capability can mean touching the whole landscape.

Composable architecture is a way to reduce that friction. It does not mean buying a random set of best-of-breed tools. It means designing the architecture around clear capability boundaries, integration principles, data ownership, and product teams that can deliver change without waiting for the entire landscape to move.

What Composable Architecture Means

Composable architecture is an approach where digital capabilities are assembled from modular components. In commerce and customer experience platforms, those components may include content management, product information, pricing, search, promotions, checkout, customer data, identity, personalisation, order management, and service capabilities.

The MACH Alliance describes composability as discrete capabilities that can be assembled, replaced, and evolved independently. MACH is often used as shorthand for microservices, API-first integration, cloud-native SaaS, and headless frontends, but the broader point is that each capability should be able to evolve without forcing every other part of the platform to change at the same pace.

That distinction matters. MACH can be a useful pattern for composable commerce and digital experience platforms, but composable architecture is bigger than a technology label. It is an architecture strategy for business change.

The Customer Experience Problem It Solves

Most organisations do not want composable architecture for its own sake. They want to launch new customer journeys without rebuilding the entire platform. They want to test a new search experience, add a marketplace capability, connect a loyalty service, introduce better content personalisation, or support a new channel without waiting for a monolithic release cycle.

McKinsey describes composable technology stacks as modular, interchangeable components that help organisations select tools for specific needs, integrate modern and legacy systems, replace underperforming tools more easily, and reduce vendor lock-in when data portability, contracts, and integration boundaries are designed well.

For customer experience, the value is not only in having more tools. The value is in orchestrating the right capabilities around the customer journey. A customer does not care whether pricing, content, search, order status, and service interactions come from different systems. The customer sees one experience. The architecture needs to make those systems work together as one coherent journey.

Composable Is a Capability Strategy, Not a Tool Shopping List

A common mistake is to treat composable architecture as a best-of-breed procurement exercise. Replace one suite with ten specialised tools, connect them quickly, and call the result composable.

That usually creates a different kind of monolith: a distributed one.

If every service has its own data model, integration style, release process, monitoring approach, and ownership model, the landscape becomes harder to change rather than easier. The organisation gains tools, but loses coherence.

The important architecture questions are not only which platform to buy, but:

  • Which business capabilities should be independent?
  • Which capabilities need to be shared across channels?
  • Which system owns which data?
  • Which APIs are product-grade and reusable?
  • Which events need to be published?
  • How do we monitor customer journeys across systems?
  • How do teams deploy without breaking each other?
  • How do we keep security, compliance, and identity consistent?

Integration and Orchestration Become the Backbone

Composable architecture moves complexity. It does not remove it. In a monolithic platform, much of the complexity sits inside the platform. In a composable landscape, more complexity sits in integration, orchestration, observability, and data consistency.

APIs need clear ownership and stable contracts. Events need consistent naming and governance. Frontends need a reliable way to consume capabilities without becoming tightly coupled to every backend. Customer data needs to flow through the landscape without creating uncontrolled copies. Monitoring needs to show the health of end-to-end journeys, not just the health of individual services.

McKinsey highlights the role of a unification or orchestration layer when many back ends and front ends need to support one connected customer experience. That idea is especially relevant in ecommerce and customer experience platforms where a website, app, service portal, store system, marketplace, or AI assistant may all need access to the same underlying capabilities.

Microservices Help, But They Are Not the Whole Answer

Microservices are often part of composable architecture, but they should not be confused with the whole strategy. Martin Fowler and James Lewis describe microservices as independently deployable services organised around business capabilities, using lightweight communication and decentralised control.

Those ideas are highly relevant to composability because they support independent change. A pricing service, product content service, search service, or checkout service can evolve with less dependency on the rest of the platform.

But microservices alone do not guarantee composability. A landscape of poorly bounded services can still be tightly coupled. A service without clear ownership can still slow down delivery. APIs without product thinking can still become fragile. Data split across services can still create inconsistency.

Moving From Monolith to Composable Without a Big Bang

For many organisations, the realistic question is not whether composable architecture is attractive. The realistic question is how to move toward it without putting the business at risk. The answer is usually incremental.

Microsoft's Azure Architecture Center describes the Strangler Fig pattern as a way to incrementally migrate a legacy system by replacing specific pieces of functionality with new applications or services while the existing system continues to run. That pattern fits composable transformation well. Instead of replacing the entire ecommerce platform or customer experience landscape in one programme, an organisation can identify the capabilities where change pressure is highest and create cleaner boundaries around them.

A practical transition might look like this:

  1. Map the current business capabilities.
  2. Identify the capabilities that limit customer experience or delivery speed.
  3. Define target ownership and integration boundaries.
  4. Introduce an orchestration or experience layer where needed.
  5. Replace or add one capability at a time.
  6. Prove the integration, monitoring, and delivery model.
  7. Expand the approach when the organisation has learned how to operate it.

When Composable Architecture Is Worth It

Composable architecture is not automatically the right answer for every organisation. It adds architectural responsibility. More modularity means more integration decisions, more vendor management, more observability requirements, and more need for product ownership.

It becomes worth it when the cost of change in the current landscape is too high. Common signals include:

  • Customer-facing improvements are delayed by platform release cycles.
  • Channels need different experiences, but share core capabilities.
  • The organisation wants to add marketplace, personalisation, search, content, loyalty, or AI capabilities without replacing the full platform.
  • One part of the suite is blocking progress, but replacing the whole suite is too risky.
  • Integration work is already happening, but without clear principles or ownership.
  • Teams cannot deploy independently because everything is coupled to the same platform release.

In those situations, composable architecture can reduce the friction of change. It can also improve cost control in specific areas by making components easier to replace and by reducing dependence on one vendor. Those benefits are not automatic. They depend on architecture quality, integration discipline, governance, and the maturity of the delivery organisation.

Common Pitfalls

Composable architecture fails when organisations focus on modular tools but ignore modular ownership. The most common failure modes are:

  • Too many tools without a target architecture.
  • APIs built for one project instead of reusable products.
  • Business capabilities split in ways that do not match real ownership.
  • Data copied across systems without a clear source of truth.
  • Frontends coupled directly to too many backends.
  • No observability across the full customer journey.
  • Governance that is either too heavy to support speed or too weak to protect coherence.

The Role of Future State Architecture

Composable architecture works best when it is connected to a clear future state architecture. That future state should describe the business capabilities, the platform layers, the integration patterns, the data ownership model, and the transition roadmap.

Without that target, composable architecture can become a collection of disconnected decisions. With it, composable architecture becomes a practical way to move from current limitations toward a more flexible customer experience platform.

The future state does not need to be perfect before the first step starts. It does need to be clear enough to guide decisions. Every new capability should move the organisation closer to the target, not create another isolated workaround.

Conclusion

Composable architecture matters because it changes how organisations deal with change. It gives businesses a way to evolve customer-facing capabilities without waiting for the whole platform to move. It supports incremental modernisation instead of risky big-bang replacement. It helps teams select and replace capabilities more deliberately.

The goal is not to build the most modular technology landscape possible. The goal is to create an architecture that lets the business keep improving the customer experience.

That requires more than MACH terminology or a list of best-of-breed tools. It requires clear business capabilities, strong integration principles, mature product teams, and a future state architecture that connects technology decisions to customer value.

Composable architecture is valuable when it helps the organisation move with less friction and more control. That is why it matters.
