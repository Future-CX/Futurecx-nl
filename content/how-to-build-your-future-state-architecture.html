How to build your Future State Architecture

Enterprise Architecture
April 26, 2025
Updated: December 1, 2025
Author
Martijn van Deel

Designing a Future State Architecture — often called a North Star Architecture — is one of the most strategic activities in any ecommerce transformation. It provides direction, reduces ambiguity, aligns stakeholders, and ensures that technology decisions contribute to long-term business goals instead of short-term patchwork fixes.

North Star Architecture Begins With Vision & Strategy

Every strong Future State or North Star Architecture starts long before any diagrams, technology choices, or integration patterns. It begins with the company’s vision and strategy.

A North Star Architecture is not simply an IT exercise — it is a translation of where the business wants to go in the next 3–5 years. Before defining systems, domains, APIs or events, you first need clarity on:

  • Who the company wants to become
  • What role does digital play in the future of the company?
  • What customer value it aims to deliver
  • How ecommerce contributes to the overall business model
  • Which capabilities are needed to support future growth

Only once the strategic direction is clear can you design an architecture that aligns with it.

Technology is not the starting point — strategy is.

Include the latest Ecommerce trends

A North Star should not be based on “gut feeling.” It must be grounded in modern, proven industry trends and principles.

  • Composable commerce (MACH)
  • Real-time Data & Event-Driven Integration
  • AI Embedded in Every Technology
  • Digital & Data-Driven Customer Experience
  • Self-Service Portals & Automation
  • Omnichannel & Headless Experiences
  • Unified Data Foundations

Incorporating these trends ensures that the architecture is future-proof, scalable, and capable of adapting to evolving customer expectations and market dynamics.

Architecture Design Principles

The North Star Architecture functions as the high-level blueprint that translates the company's vision and strategy into a coherent technical direction. It provides the structure and boundaries within which solution architects make detailed design choices. The folliwng guiding principles help teams evaluate options, validate decisions, and ensure that every solution remains faithful to the long-term intent of the North Star.

1. Buy before Build

Many of the required functionalities in the new platform are standard solutions commonly used by other companies and offered by vendors as part of their products. These standard functionalities provide reliable and proven solutions, and multiple vendors offer configurable off-the-shelf products.

This principle guides the decision-making process for solution selection.

2. Configure before Customize

By selecting vendors that offer flexible configuration options, the architecture remains adaptable. Configuration enables efficient scalability and supports future extensions, whereas customizations often lead to higher costs and increased complexity.

Using configurable solutions allows for faster implementations and easier maintenance.

This principle defines how the selected solutions are implemented and utilized.

3. Innovate to Differentiate

Custom-built components can bring unique capabilities to market and help differentiate Bidfood from competitors. The focus should be on developing distinctive features that deliver a competitive advantage. Investing in innovative technologies and processes drives growth and customer loyalty, while prioritizing research and development ensures continuous improvement and differentiation.

This principle guides where to invest in custom features and functionalities.

The future commerce platform will be designed according to modern best practices for composable architecture, ensuring that each capability can evolve independently while still working seamlessly as part of a unified digital ecosystem.

4. Modular

Instead of relying on an all-in-one (monolithic) solution, the future architecture will consist of separate modules or components, each serving a specific function.

5. Cloud-based

The new commerce platform will run in the cloud, partly using SaaS solutions and public cloud services, and partly on custom-built components that are cloud-hosted.

6. Loosely coupled

By avoiding tight integrations and using asynchronous integrations wherever possible, the dependencies between components are reduced.

Why Composable Architecture Matters

Composable architecture allows businesses to innovate faster, scale smarter, and adapt rapidly by assembling modular, replaceable components that evolve independently.

 

Five Key Objectives

Five key objectives emerge from our ambition, business goals, and the trends we see in the market. Together, they define the direction and purpose of our North Star Architecture.

Respond quickly to market developments through a layered and composable architecture.

We want to be able to respond quickly to rapidly changing customer expectations and market dynamics, while also having the freedom to introduce new services and run experiments without disrupting the core landscape.

To achieve this, we are restructuring the architecture into a horizontally layered, composable model in which capabilities can be reused across multiple channels. By decoupling channels from services, business processes, and data, we can build functionality once and make it available everywhere—reducing duplication, speeding up delivery, and ensuring consistency.

Centralising the management of shared functionalities, processes, and data keeps the IT landscape clean and lean, lowering complexity and increasing the organisation’s ability to adapt. Because each component in a composable architecture is modular and replaceable, new capabilities can be added—or outdated ones removed—without impacting the rest of the ecosystem. Through well-defined APIs, these modular components can be integrated seamlessly into different channels and processes, enabling us to innovate and scale with far greater agility.

The layers help differentiate in pace according to the well-known Bi-Modal IT concept or Gartner's “Pace-Layered Application Strategy” model.

Systems of Innovation These are applications with the shortest lifecycle, often less than a year. They are typically newly developed solutions created to meet emerging customer needs or to address urgent business requirements.

Systems of Differentiation These applications have a medium lifecycle of one to three years and are often used across the organization. They are regularly adapted or updated to respond to industry changes or shifts in business policy.

Systems of Record These applications have a long lifecycle, often ten years or more, and are usually the most stable systems. They manage critical master data and support core business processes, which makes organizations more cautious when considering changes to them.

Ensure uniformity and consistency across all channels through an Engagement Services layer.

Customers expect consistent, reliable, and accurate information across every channel they use. To deliver an experience that feels predictable and error-free, we need to ensure that all channels operate from the same processes, rules, and data.

To achieve this consistency, we are introducing a centralized Engagement Services layer that orchestrates customer-facing processes across all channels. By consolidating the capabilities that shape the customer experience—such as customer profiles, pricing logic, availability checks, promotions, and order-related flows—we can ensure that every interaction is aligned, regardless of whether the customer engages through web, mobile, app, or future channels.

Moving these functions and customer data away from individual channel applications and the Enterprise Foundation into the Engagement Services layer allows us to build processes once and reuse them everywhere. This eliminates channel-specific logic, reduces errors, and ensures that customers always see the same information.

Centralizing engagement services also generates deeper insights into customer behaviour and journeys. With all interactions flowing through a unified layer, we can learn faster, identify pain points earlier, and better anticipate evolving customer needs—ultimately strengthening the overall customer experience.

Seamless integration of partner services using market standards and APIs.

To deliver a richer customer experience and accelerate innovation, we need the ability to collaborate effortlessly with external partners, SaaS platforms, and new digital services. This requires seamless, secure, and scalable connectivity—without introducing complexity or slowing down the business.

We achieve this by adopting an open architecture that relies on industry standards and API-first integration. This approach allows us to onboard partners and external services quickly while ensuring that connectivity remains stable, secure, and maintainable over time.

A composable architecture further strengthens this by making it easy to embed partner capabilities directly into our core processes—whether it’s product information, customer data, analytics, promotions, fulfilment, or channel experiences. By integrating through well-defined APIs, partner services can flow naturally into our PIM, CDP, and engagement layers, enabling us to offer customers more value without heavy customisation or point-to-point complexity.

This approach not only accelerates partner adoption but also ensures that our ecosystem remains flexible, future-proof, and ready for new opportunities.

A relevant and personalized experience achieved by combining a 360° customer view with real-time data.

To truly serve customers in a meaningful way, we need to recognise who they are, understand their context, and respond with experiences that fit their needs in the moment. This requires more than segmentation—it requires a unified, real-time understanding of each customer across all touchpoints.

We achieve this by developing a 360° customer view enriched with data from every channel. By combining operational, behavioural, and transactional insights into one unified profile, we can link real-time customer behaviour and events to current and planned activities. This enables us to deliver experiences that are consistently relevant, timely, and personalised.

On top of this foundation, we integrate a Customer Data Platform (CDP) that activates the 360° view across all channels. The CDP becomes the single source for orchestrating personalisation, recommendations, journeys, and triggered interactions, ensuring that customers receive consistent and proactive engagement—whether they visit the website, use the app, or interact through future channels.

The underlying data foundation acts as the central repository for all operational and transactional data. By making this data accessible through the CDP, every channel can benefit from the same insights, creating a unified, intelligent, and context-aware customer experience.

Enable intelligent processes by centralizing data from all channels and systems.

To make intelligent, data-driven decisions, we need a complete and consistent view of what is happening across all channels. Centralising data enables us to connect the dots, recognise patterns, and power smarter processes that improve both customer experience and operational outcomes.

We realise this objective by managing data efficiently and consistently within the Enterprise Foundation layer, where it becomes easily accessible to all processes, services, and channels that rely on fact-based decision-making. By fully leveraging our data foundation—storing, enriching, and organising data from every channel—we create a comprehensive, reliable dataset for analysis, modelling, reporting, and dashboards.

This central data foundation ensures that all applications and services operate with the same version of the truth, reducing inconsistency and enabling unified, cross-channel insights. As part of this foundation, we establish a real-time data framework for processing journey events. This allows us to quickly detect changes in customer behaviour, share relevant insights across the landscape, and trigger intelligent, data-driven processes.

With consistent, accessible, and real-time data at the core, we can support smarter decisions, enhance responsiveness, and create a more predictive and customer-centric organisation.

Next is the Solution Blueprint

Now that the North Star design principles are defined, we can create a North Star Solution Blueprint for our new eCommerce landscape.

This blueprint will provide a high-level overview of the capabilities required, the solutions in place or planned, and the data and integrations needed — all visualized in a comprehensive solution architecture diagram.

1. We start with the layers...

We begin by defining the layers based on a composable architecture, enabling flexibility and agility to quickly respond to evolving customer needs and market changes, and to introduce new services through experimentation.

To achieve this, we are restructuring our architecture into a horizontally layered, composable model where solutions can be reused across multiple channels.

2. ... then add the Capabilities...

We add the required capabilities and position them within the appropriate layers. Where possible, we indicate the hosting model — whether SaaS, PaaS, or On-Premise.

Centralizing the capabilities that shape the customer experience across channels is essential to ensure consistent omnichannel interactions.

3. ... and the Solutions.

We include the solution names that deliver each business function — noting which solutions are existing, planned, or yet to be determined.

4. Then we add the Data...

We include the key data objects required for each solution to operate and indicate their data ownership — with orange representing data mastered within the solution and yellow indicating data sourced from another system.

5. ... and the Integrations.

Now that we know which data objects each solution requires and where the data originates, we can define the necessary integrations. Our starting point is to use market standards and APIs within an open architecture approach.

 

 

 

 

Ready for more details

Now that we have drafted a high-level Solution Blueprint, we can deep dive into the Integration Designs.

Using the defined Integration Principles and Integration Patterns, we will explore key data objects — such as Users, Products, and Orders — and develop Integration Architecture Designs for each.

From these Integration Designs, we can develop detailed Solution Architecture Designs for each solution — such as Commerce, CIAM, CDP, and Promotions.

At this stage, we have a clear understanding of the data each solution requires, how that data is sourced, and the relationships and interactions between the different solutions within the landscape.

 

Summary

Building a Future State or North Star Architecture is far more than drawing a target diagram — it is the strategic translation of a company’s vision, ambition, and long-term direction into a coherent, modern, and composable e-commerce landscape.

By grounding the design in business strategy, aligning with the latest industry trends, and applying clear architectural principles, organisations can create a platform that is flexible, consistent across channels, open for partner innovation, deeply data-driven, and capable of delivering personalised and intelligent customer experiences at scale.

With the five key objectives defined and the architecture anchored in a layered, modular, API-first and cloud-ready model, we now have a solid foundation for the Solution Blueprint — the next step where capabilities, solutions, data, and integrations come together into a unified, actionable architectural view.
